CAA in Odisha Begins, Refugees Cheer, Local Concerns Remain

Published on December 12, 2025

Follow Us:

X Facebook Instagram Threads
On December 11, 2025, Odisha formally began implementing the Citizenship Amendment Act CAA 2019 by issuing Indian citizenship certificates to 35 Bangladeshi Hindu refugees at a ceremony in Bhubaneswar. Chief Minister Mohan Charan Majhi handed over the certificates, calling the move a humanitarian step for people who had faced religious persecution and lived in India for decades without legal status.

This was the first batch of citizenship grants under CAA in Odisha, following the notification of CAA rules by the central government in March 2024.

Who Received Citizenship and Why

The 35 beneficiaries are Hindu migrants from Bangladesh, most of whom have been living in Nabarangpur district and nearby areas since the late 1990s and early 2000s. Many families migrated during periods of communal violence in Bangladesh and settled in southern Odisha as agricultural labourers and small farmers.

Despite long residence, they remained stateless for years. Without citizenship, they could not vote, own land legally, or fully access welfare schemes such as housing, ration cards, and caste based benefits. The citizenship certificates now give them full legal rights as Indian citizens.

What the Law Provides

The Citizenship Amendment Act allows fast track citizenship for non Muslim minorities from Bangladesh, Pakistan, and Afghanistan who entered India before the notified cut off date. Applications are submitted online and examined by district and state level committees, with final approval granted by empowered committees under the Ministry of Home Affairs.

States like Odisha mainly facilitate document verification and coordination, while decisions are taken by the Centre.

Limited Numbers, Large Backlog

While the government highlighted the event as historic, the scale of implementation remains small. Officials have said that around 1,100 CAA applications are currently pending in Odisha, with only 51 citizenship certificates issued so far.

Critics question why the process has moved slowly despite the law being passed in 2019 and rules notified in 2024. No clear timeline has been announced for clearing the remaining applications, raising concerns among long settled families who continue to wait.

Welfare Promises and Administrative Capacity

At the ceremony, Revenue and Disaster Management Minister Suresh Pujari said land would be allocated to landless families and caste certificates issued quickly. However, the government has not released detailed timelines or budget allocations for rehabilitation.

Odisha already faces delays in land settlement, housing approvals, and welfare delivery, especially in tribal and backward districts. Civil society groups argue that without clear planning, citizenship alone may not translate into real improvements on the ground.

Impact on Odisha’s Existing Challenges

Odisha continues to struggle with unemployment, malnutrition, and land rights issues, particularly in districts like Nabarangpur, Koraput, and Malkangiri. Tribal land alienation remains a sensitive issue in scheduled areas.

Critics have raised concerns that allocating land and welfare benefits to new citizens, without expanding overall resources, could increase pressure on already stretched systems. They argue that the state has yet to fully resolve land disputes and displacement affecting Adivasi communities.

Employment and Social Integration Questions

Rural unemployment and seasonal migration remain major problems in Odisha. Integrating new citizens into schemes like MGNREGA and housing programmes may provide relief to refugee families, but experts warn that without parallel job creation, competition for limited opportunities could grow.

So far, Odisha has not seen major protests over CAA, unlike some other states. However, analysts caution that linking citizenship to religion can have long term social effects, even if immediate tensions are low.

Centre Led Process, State Level Messaging

Although the Odisha government led the public ceremony, the citizenship approvals are controlled by the central government. Opposition parties argue that while the state takes political credit, it avoids accountability for delays, transparency, and rehabilitation outcomes.

They also point out that other persecuted groups not covered under CAA remain excluded, raising questions about selective humanitarianism.

A Mixed Outcome for Odisha

For the 35 families, citizenship brings long awaited dignity and legal security. For Odisha as a state, however, the impact is more complex.

The rollout highlights unresolved questions about administrative capacity, land availability, welfare delivery, and policy priorities. Until the government publishes clear data, timelines, and rehabilitation plans, critics say the CAA implementation in Odisha remains a symbolic start rather than a fully thought out solution.

The real test, they argue, will be whether Odisha can balance humanitarian commitments with its pressing local issues of land rights, employment, and social cohesion.
