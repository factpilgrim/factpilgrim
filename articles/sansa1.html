Apple’s Pushback and Public Outrage Force Government Retreat on Sanchar Saathi Mandate

Published on December 3, 2025

A major digital policy storm erupted after a confidential government directive surfaced requiring smartphone makers to preload the Sanchar Saathi app on all new devices. The order, intended to strengthen telecom security and raise awareness about fraud, quickly ignited nationwide debate. Concerns over privacy, consent, and state overreach dominated public discourse, and the issue escalated further when Apple refused to comply, pushing the government to soften its stance.

The Order

The directive, issued on November 28, 2025, instructed manufacturers to preload the Sanchar Saathi app within 90 days and ensure it could not be disabled or deleted by users. It was framed as a measure to help citizens identify fraudulent SIM activity, track lost phones, and report cyber fraud. However, the non-removable requirement raised immediate alarms. Many questioned why an app meant for user protection needed compulsory installation and elevated privileges on personal devices.

The Outrage

Once details of the mandate became public, criticism mounted rapidly. Digital rights advocates warned that forcing a government app onto millions of phones without user control threatened digital privacy. Social media platforms filled with concerns about possible surveillance, misuse of access, and erosion of personal autonomy. Opposition parties demanded clarity in Parliament, calling the decision intrusive and unnecessary. The growing public distrust added significant pressure on the government to respond.

Apple's Denial

Apple became the central player in the controversy by refusing to follow the new requirement. The company argued that its iOS ecosystem does not permit third party apps to be granted system level privileges or installed without user consent. Preloading a non-removable app, Apple said, directly contradicts its security architecture and would increase the risk of security breaches. Apple communicated its stance privately to the government but remained firm, citing long standing commitment to device integrity and user privacy.

The U Turn

Under intensifying public scrutiny and industry resistance, the government shifted its position. On December 2, 2025, Telecom Minister Jyotiraditya Scindia clarified that Sanchar Saathi is optional, fully deletable, and not designed for surveillance. He emphasized that the government only intended to encourage awareness, not impose an undeletable application. This clarification marked a retreat from the original mandate and was viewed as a response to both public concern and Apple's refusal.

Sanchar Saathi serves an important purpose in combating telecom fraud, and users who find value in it will install it voluntarily. A beneficial and protective app gains adoption on its own merit. Mandating it, especially as a non-removable tool, undermines trust and fuels suspicion. In matters of digital security, transparency and user consent must guide policy, not compulsion.
