Is The Modi Government Trying To Spy On All Mobile Users? The Sanchar Saathi Controversy Explained

Published on December 2, 2025
A national debate has erupted after the government ordered Sanchar Saathi to be installed on every new smartphone sold in India. Critics call it a quiet attempt to put a government controlled app inside every citizen’s pocket. Supporters call it an essential tool against rising cybercrime. The question now is simple and serious: is this policy about public safety or surveillance power?

Why Your Phone Data Is The Ultimate Surveillance Tool

A smartphone holds the most intimate map of your life. It stores calls, chats, photos, locations, browsing habits and your unique IMEI number. Anyone who controls this data can track your behaviour, beliefs and movements. With cyber fraud expected to cross Rs 20,000 crore in 2025, leaked phone data has already become one of India’s biggest vulnerabilities.

What Sanchar Saathi Actually Is And Who Runs It

Sanchar Saathi is a government app created by the Department of Telecommunications. It helps block stolen phones, report spam, check linked SIMs and verify IMEI numbers. On 28 November 2025, the government ordered that every new phone must carry this app permanently. It cannot be removed and updates will be forced through the system.

Why Critics Say This Mandate Enables Mass Surveillance

The app needs access to calls, SMS, location, storage and potentially camera data. If this access is misused, agencies can track citizens in real time or create behavioural profiles. Making it compulsory removes user choice and removes the ability to limit permissions. This raises the fear that the state may be building a phone level monitoring infrastructure.

Why The Government Says It Is Not Surveillance

Officials argue the goal is to stop phone cloning, fraud networks and rising cybercrime. They say the app helps police find stolen devices faster and protects users who do not understand digital risks. The government insists that no routine data collection happens and the app is for public safety.

Why The Sudden Decision Without Any Consultation

The order was issued with no public debate or industry feedback. The government claims urgency due to rising fraud. Critics say this was intentional to avoid scrutiny and label the move an example of unchecked executive power. The 90 day deadline for manufacturers suggests a rushed rollout.

Past Incidents That Explain The Timing

Cybercrime has surged. These numbers show why the government says stronger controls are needed, but also why some fear overreach disguised as protection.

Cybercrime and Fraud Trend by Year:

Year Key Trend Summary
2022 2.5 lakh fraud cases and approx Rs 7,500 crore losses, dominated by SIM swaps and loan scams
2023 4 lakh cases and approx Rs 11,000 crore losses, major rise in WhatsApp identity fraud
2024 7.4 lakh cases and Rs 11,000 plus crore losses, 369 million malware hits and deepfake scams worth Rs 100 crore
2025 Fraud losses projected between Rs 20,000 crore and Rs 1.2 lakh crore, 13,000 bank frauds in FY25

These patterns reflect a rapid escalation that the government wants to counter. The question is whether the chosen method is justified.

Past Government Tech Pushes That Raised Privacy Flags

India has seen similar debates. Aarogya Setu in 2020, the 2023 push to regulate pre-installed apps and large scale bans on foreign apps all triggered concerns about increasing digital control. Courts and activists have repeatedly warned that such measures can quietly expand surveillance capability.

Common Phone Mistakes That Now Carry More Risk

Forwarding unverified messages, tapping phishing links, sharing private material, using unapproved VPNs or ignoring security updates can lead to legal trouble under IT and DPDP rules. With a mandatory government app on every device, many worry that mistakes may be flagged faster and monitored more closely.

How Users Can Protect Themselves

Verify messages, use official apps only, limit permissions wherever possible, clean sensitive data and report fraud promptly. These habits reduce legal and security risks even in a more tightly monitored digital environment.

The Sanchar Saathi mandate has turned into a national test of trust. Supporters call it protection. Critics call it preparation for mass surveillance. Whether this policy becomes a security milestone or a privacy crisis will depend on how much power the state decides to exercise through the device that every citizen carries in their hand.
