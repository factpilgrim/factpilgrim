Odisha Govt Faces Heat Over ₹1.6 Crore Deal With Madhuri Dixit

Published on December 3, 2025

Follow Us:

X Facebook Instagram Threads

Odisha's decision to bring in Bollywood star Madhuri Dixit as brand ambassador for its handloom sector has triggered significant public outrage. The State Assembly recently confirmed that the one-year contract cost Rs 1.6 crore, excluding travel and stay. Intended to elevate iconic weaves like Bomkai, Pasapalli, Sambalpuri and Ikat, which support over 7 lakh weavers contributing nearly Rs 3,000 crore annually, the campaign has instead become a major political and social talking point.

Concerns Over Impact and Execution

The government hoped a national celebrity would attract new markets and spotlight Odisha's weaving traditions. But expectations of a robust, sustained promotional campaign have not materialised. The gap between the investment and visible output is now driving widespread public anger and demands for accountability.

One Reel and No Follow-Up

Since her appointment in August 2025, Madhuri Dixit has uploaded only a single Instagram reel tagged #ad. No further posts, events, collaborations or promotional engagements have been seen across any platform. The phrase "Rs 1.6 crore for one reel" has become a defining criticism, with many questioning whether the campaign is being monitored or delivered as promised.

Cost Under Scrutiny

The Rs 1.6 crore fee was reportedly paid upfront from public funds, with travel, accommodation and shoot requirements billed separately. Critics argue that spending such a large amount feels disconnected from ground realities where many weavers still face low income, limited market reach and inconsistent demand. The imbalance between celebrity spending and artisan hardships has intensified the debate.

Public Mood Turning Negative

Social media is dominated by critical commentary.

  • Many feel a celebrity with no cultural link to Odisha cannot authentically represent its handloom heritage.
  • Suggestions are pouring in to instead promote local designers, artisans or Odia public figures who better represent the craft.
  • Others accuse the government of chasing glamour rather than strengthening genuine support systems for weavers.
  • Even the small group that supports using a national face admits the price and execution appear poorly justified.

The issue has now shifted from a promotional misstep to a broader conversation on governance, planning and responsible spending of public money.

Odisha's handloom tradition is strong, authentic and capable of drawing admiration on its own merit. When a promotional effort is meaningful and genuinely supports artisans, people naturally connect with it. But relying on costly endorsements without clear output does not guarantee success. A culturally aligned and grounded approach will always make a deeper impact than a high-priced single reel.
